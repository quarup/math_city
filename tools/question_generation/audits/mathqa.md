# MathQA — ingestion audit

This document is the input to the dataset-ingestion sub-track decisions
for MathQA. It examines every `(category, sub_bucket)` bucket, samples
real items, and assigns a verdict per bucket:

- **variety** — the underlying math is already covered by our algorithmic
  generators; ingesting adds *phrasing variety*.
- **gap-fill** — the math isn't covered by any current generator. High
  value to ingest (subject to text-quality + format complexity).
- **skip** — within K–8 scope in principle but per-item filter cost
  (text cleanup, HS-level filtering, malformed-options handling) is too
  high relative to the marginal pedagogical lift.
- **out_of_scope** — outside CCSS K–8 (HS algebra / trig / log /
  factorial / physics framing).

The raw sampled items live in [mathqa_samples.md](mathqa_samples.md),
auto-regenerated by [`audit_mathqa.py`](../audit_mathqa.py).

**License context.** MathQA itself is Apache 2.0 (Allen AI) — no license
blocker. The verdict turns entirely on content quality and curriculum
fit.

---

## Headline finding

**MathQA's value proposition does not hold up under inspection.** The
dataset's stated draw (per [curriculum.md §7.2](../../curriculum.md))
was "37K cleaned multiple-choice algebra problems with executable
formula annotations (gold for free distractor generation and answer
verification)." Three claims, evaluated against the samples:

1. **"Cleaned"** — *no*. Text quality is poor across the corpus:
   aggressive tokenization (`"rs . 400"`, `"a ' s amount"`,
   `"95 m * 55 m"`), Unicode artifacts (`"â ˆ š"` for √, `"ã —"` for ×),
   no sentence casing, "tough and tricky questions :" preambles.
   Roughly every other prompt would need rewriting for a kids' app.
2. **"Executable formula annotations"** — *unreliable*. The
   `annotated_formula` field was generated by an automated tagger that
   frequently mis-classifies items. The `(general, geom_2d_encoded)`
   bucket is the canonical demonstration: every sample is non-geometry
   ("what is 5 + 7", "the largest 4 digit number exactly divisible by
   90 is ?") but tagged with `circle_area(...)` or `square_area(...)`.
   Same noise in `(other, geom_2d_encoded)` — all "what is the square
   root of N?" items tagged with `circle_area(...)`. Treating the
   formula as a trustworthy signal would propagate the tagger's errors
   into our content.
3. **"Gold for distractor generation and answer verification"** —
   *no*. The `options` field is often malformed (`"<missing-option:c>"`
   appears across many buckets, where the parser couldn't find the
   labelled letter); "none of these" is a valid correct answer in a
   non-trivial fraction of items (unusable for our format, since it
   carries no distractor signal); and the `correct` letter sometimes
   points to a prefixed-label string (`"b ) 216"`, `"a . 240"`) rather
   than a clean value.

Within the cleanest cells (`(geometry, geom_2d_encoded)`,
`(geometry, geom_3d_encoded)`, `(geometry, arith_sqrt)`) the content is
useful and on-curriculum, but even there ~5–10% of items have
malformed options or HS-level constructions, so a manual cleanup pass
is unavoidable.

**Net recommendation:** **skip broad ingestion. Optionally ingest a
narrow geometry slice (~900 items) for *variety* on top of our existing
G3–G8 area / perimeter / volume / Pythagorean generators — but only if
the cleanup cost is paid up front (text normalization + manual filter
for `none-of-these` / `missing-option` / HS items).** The cost-benefit
trade is borderline; my recommendation is to **defer the geometry
ingest** until we've shipped GSM8K + SVAMP and have measured real-world
exposure to repetitive phrasing in the geometry generators. If users
hit the same geometry prompts repeatedly, then come back and ingest the
geometry slice.

## Why the broad-skip verdict, not a per-bucket cherry-pick

The DeepMind audit produced a per-submodule mix of verdicts (variety /
gap-fill / out_of_scope). MathQA's situation is different in a way
that pushes the whole-dataset verdict toward skip:

1. **Text quality is a corpus-wide problem, not a per-bucket one.** The
   tokenization (`"rs . 400"`, `"a ' s"`), the Unicode garbage
   (`"â ˆ š"`), the lack of sentence casing, and the "tough and tricky
   questions :" preambles run through every category. Any ingest needs
   a corpus-wide text-normalization pass before the per-item filter.
   That's a sunk cost regardless of how many buckets we ingest.

2. **The formula annotations are too noisy to drive distractor
   generation.** This was the headline value-add when we put MathQA on
   the priority list. The samples kill it: every `geom_2d_encoded`
   bucket outside `category=geometry` is fabricated nonsense; even
   inside `category=geometry`, the formulas sometimes nest 5+ ops for
   problems whose K–8 solution is one step. We can't trust the formula
   as a verification or distractor signal.

3. **The cleanest slices are *variety* on math we already cover.**
   `(geometry, geom_2d_encoded)`, `(geometry, geom_3d_encoded)`,
   `(geometry, arith_sqrt)` are ~1,300 items. After filtering for
   `none-of-these`, `missing-option`, HS-level radicands, and nested-
   formula complexity, the usable yield is ~500–700. Spread across ~10
   sub-concepts (G3–G8 area / perimeter / volume / Pythagorean), that's
   50–70 items per concept — modest variety on top of our existing
   algorithmic generators, which already produce unbounded items per
   concept.

4. **The two §7.2 datasets we haven't audited yet — GSM8K (#2) and
   SVAMP (#5) — are much higher-leverage.** GSM8K is 8,500 grade-school
   word problems with reliable step-by-step rationales (single source,
   single license, clean text). SVAMP is 1,000 grade-2–4 word problems
   confirmed clean from the MD-ES sample inspection. Both are
   substantially cleaner and on-curriculum for grades MathQA doesn't
   reach. Engineer-hours are better spent there.

The MathQA audit is thus the second negative result in the §7.7 series
(MD-ES was the first, on license grounds). It removes the "37K
multiple-choice items with formula verification" claim from our
mental model: the items are present, but the verification is unreliable
and the items themselves need substantial cleanup.

## What a narrow MathQA ingest would look like (if we do it later)

For completeness, sketch of the path back to ingesting MathQA if
post-launch play exposes repetitive geometry phrasing in our
generators:

1. Build a text-normalizer: strip "tough and tricky questions :"
   preambles; fix common Unicode (`â ˆ š` → `√`, `ã —` → `×`,
   `’` → `'`); restore decimal points in `rs . 400`; capitalize
   sentence starts; trim leading/trailing whitespace.
2. Filter items where `correct ∈ {"none of these", "none"}` or the
   labelled option text is `"none of these"`.
3. Filter items where the option parser returns `<missing-option:X>`.
4. Restrict to `(category, sub_bucket) ∈ {(geometry, geom_2d_encoded),
   (geometry, geom_3d_encoded), (geometry, arith_sqrt)}`.
5. Restrict to formulas that use a single encoded op (no nesting > 2
   levels) — drops the over-complex items.
6. For `(geometry, arith_sqrt)`: require the radicand to be a perfect
   square (K–8 sqrt scope).
7. Map each filtered item to a curriculum.md sub-concept via the
   formula's outermost encoded op (`rectangle_area` →
   `area_rectangle_*`, `volume_cube` → `volume_cube_formula`, etc.).
8. Run the resulting JSON through our existing dataset-ingestion
   pipeline (per the schema in
   [tools/question_generation/README.md](../README.md)).
9. Generate 3 distractors per item by varying the operand by ±10–20%
   (we can't trust the existing `options` field, since 3 of the 5
   options are wrong by design and we don't have a reliable way to
   pick which 3 of those wrongs are pedagogically interesting).

Estimated yield: ~500 items across ~10 G3–G8 geometry sub-concepts.
Estimated effort: 1 chunk (text-normalizer + filter pipeline) + 1
chunk (mapping + ingest). Defer until needed.

## Recommended next audit

Promote **GSM8K** (#2 on §7.2) to the front of the queue. SVAMP (#5) is
the smallest and confirmed-clean from the MD-ES sample inspection
(audit will be very quick); GSM8K is the highest-leverage open
dataset for grades 3–6 word problems and the only one we haven't yet
got a read on. Both should be audited before any MathQA ingest happens.
